# Reframed Manuscript Skeleton

# Globally conserved measles virus therapeutic pockets define a structure-guided repurposing framework in the context of re-emergence in Mexico and Latin America

## Central Hypothesis

Despite heterogeneous regional surveillance and lineage turnover, measles virus preserves structurally actionable small-molecule pockets in prefusion F and the L polymerase complex that remain sufficiently conserved across regional and global sequence space to support rational antiviral repurposing. The recent re-emergence of measles in Mexico and Latin America provides the epidemiologic rationale for this search, but therapeutic target definition should be anchored in a broader regional-global comparative framework rather than in local sequence density alone.

## Overall Objective

To identify and prioritize conserved, structurally defined measles virus therapeutic pockets and to establish a docking-ready repurposing framework grounded in curated regional and global sequence evidence.

## Specific Aims

1. Define the epidemiologic and surveillance rationale for antiviral preparedness by positioning the recent resurgence in Mexico and Latin America as the regional context for a broader therapeutic search.
2. Curate a regional-global measles virus sequence framework and quantify the conservation of docking-relevant residues in the prefusion F pocket and the L polymerase pocket.
3. Translate structure and conservation evidence into a therapeutic repurposing workflow that prioritizes L as the primary docking campaign, F as the orthogonal secondary campaign, and explicit structural comparators as pocket validators.

## Introduction

Measles virus remains one of the most transmissible human pathogens and continues to produce outbreaks wherever immunity gaps intersect with importation and under-immunized population clusters. The recent resurgence observed in Mexico and across the Americas shows that elimination-era expectations do not remove the need for outbreak preparedness. In this context, the regional signal is scientifically important not only because of its public health burden, but also because it reopens the question of whether antiviral preparedness should complement vaccination-centered control strategies.

Vaccination remains the dominant tool for measles prevention, yet no approved direct antiviral has been incorporated into routine clinical or outbreak management practice. That gap matters most in severe disease, in vulnerable hosts, and in response settings where therapeutic support could reinforce clinical management or containment. A structure-guided antiviral strategy is therefore justified as a preparedness effort rather than as a substitute for immunization.

Recent structural work has materially changed the actionable target space of measles virus. The prefusion fusion glycoprotein contains a defined inhibitor-accessible cavity, and the L polymerase complex now includes ligand-engaged conformations that directly connect pocket architecture with antiviral mechanism. Together, these advances make it possible to ask a more relevant translational question: whether the most druggable measles virus sites are conserved enough across currently accessible sequence space to support rational repurposing.

That question cannot be answered from a purely local lens. Public sequence surveillance is uneven across regions, and the recent Mexican outbreak is not matched by dense public deposition of genome-scale viral sequences. Consequently, any therapeutic target definition built only around Mexican records would be underpowered and potentially misleading. Mexico and Latin America should therefore serve primarily as the outbreak-relevance layer, whereas target definition should be anchored in a broader regional-global framework.

Here, a curated sequence-to-structure strategy was adopted to resolve that problem. Mexico and Latin America were retained as the epidemiologic and surveillance context, N was used to preserve phylogenetic structure, and F plus L were advanced as the principal docking-oriented targets. Under this design, the central aim was not to infer uniquely Mexican therapeutic liabilities, but to identify globally conserved measles virus pockets with immediate relevance to regional resurgence and with a credible path toward antiviral repurposing.


## Results

### Regional outbreak relevance motivated antiviral preparedness rather than local target definition

Official surveillance documents showed that the measles resurgence in Mexico expanded rapidly from focal detection to broad territorial dissemination in the setting of suboptimal two-dose vaccine coverage. This finding justified the clinical and public health relevance of an antiviral preparedness framework, but it did not by itself define the therapeutic target space. In the revised manuscript logic, the Mexican and Latin American outbreak signal functions as the motivating context for the study rather than as the sole molecular inference layer.

### Public sequence availability supported a regional-global analytic design

Exact curation of GenBank source metadata showed that the public Mexican sequence space remained sparse and historically skewed. Only 10 unique Mexican N-gene records were retained after exact geographic filtering, whereas no Mexican F sequences and no Mexican L sequences were available in the curated dataset. By contrast, Latin American exact records yielded 138 N sequences, 10 F sequences, and 14 L sequences, and the global complete-genome context contributed 405 N, 432 F, and 630 L sequences. The latest curated Mexican public record dated to 2019, indicating that the recent outbreak was not represented by publicly retrievable Mexican genome-scale sequence data in the dataset fetched on June 6, 2026. This evidence justified a split design in which regional outbreak relevance remained visible, but docking-oriented conservation was defined using a broader Latin American plus global framework.

Historical public Mexican records still documented repeated genotype detections rather than a single stable endemic lineage, which preserved the relevance of regional molecular context while arguing against over-weighting it in the main therapeutic narrative. For that reason, the Mexico-specific molecular history was retained as supportive material rather than as the central organizing axis of the Results section.

### Structurally actionable therapeutic space converged on F and the polymerase complex

Integration of viral biology with public structural evidence resolved the measles virus target space into an entry module and a replication module. H and F define the entry axis, but only F currently provides a structurally explicit small-molecule-accessible pocket in a target-engaged prefusion state. The replication axis is defined by the L-P machinery and the nucleocapsid scaffold, yet the most intervention-ready structural evidence is concentrated in polymerase-engaged assemblies that include ERDRP-0519-bound holo complexes. These data converged on F and L as the leading structure-guided targets for a repurposing-oriented campaign.

### Docking-relevant F and L pockets were highly conserved across curated sequence space

Pocket-directed conservation analysis showed that both the prefusion F inhibitor cavity and the ERDRP-0519-defined L polymerase cavity were highly conserved across the curated Latin American plus global sequence panels. All 13 F-contact residues mapped from 5YZC remained at or above 98.57% consensus frequency, and only residues 453 and 458 showed minor variability. Likewise, all 31 L-contact residues mapped from 9OCF remained at or above 98.92% consensus frequency, with only residues 535, 544, and 739 falling below complete invariance. Structural superposition further supported the use of ligand-engaged reference states: 5YZC and 5YZD aligned at 0.2933 angstrom RMSD, and 9OCF and 9OCE aligned at 0.1170 angstrom RMSD, whereas the ATP-bound 9OCH state was conformationally distinct from the ERDRP-0519-bound polymerase conformation. Together, these findings indicate that the selected F and L pockets are not only structurally defined, but also exceptionally stable across the accessible sequence space relevant to therapeutic targeting.

### Conservation and structural evidence defined the repurposing workflow

A transparent prioritization rubric integrating essential viral role, structural tractability, target-engaged complexes, and published antiviral mechanism placed the L polymerase pocket at the front of the computational workflow and the prefusion F pocket in the orthogonal secondary position. This ordering translated directly into the docking design. The repurposing library was framed around clinically interpretable small molecules, whereas ERDRP-0519, AS-48, and FIP were retained as structural comparators to validate pocket recognition rather than to inflate the translational shortlist. In this reframed narrative, the core result is therefore not Mexico-specific target definition, but the identification of globally conserved measles virus therapeutic pockets with immediate regional relevance and a rational entry point for drug repurposing.
